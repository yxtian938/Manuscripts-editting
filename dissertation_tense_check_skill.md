# Dissertation Tense Check Skill

## Purpose

Use this skill to audit verb tense in an English-language dissertation, especially in applied linguistics, education, social science, humanities, and empirical research writing. The skill focuses on simple past, present perfect, past perfect, and the other tense-aspect forms that commonly interact with them.

The governing principle is the **Reference-Point Model**: tense is not only about calendar time. In dissertation writing, tense tells the reader whether a clause refers to a completed research act, a still-relevant body of knowledge, a live theoretical claim, a textual object currently being discussed, or an event earlier than another past event.

Do not force one tense across an entire chapter. Tense must be checked clause by clause according to rhetorical function.

---

## Empirical baseline from dissertation tense research

An open-source corpus study of six humanities and science dissertations manually counted **10,021 finite verb forms**. The distribution was:

| Tense-aspect form | Occurrences | Frequency |
|---|---:|---:|
| Simple present | 4,247 | 42.4% |
| Simple past | 3,943 | 39.3% |
| Present progressive | 914 | 9.1% |
| Present perfect | 561 | 5.6% |
| Past perfect | 168 | 1.7% |
| Simple future | 150 | 1.5% |
| Past progressive | 38 | 0.3% |
| **Total** | **10,021** | **100%** |

The main implication is that **simple present and simple past are the core tense pair in dissertation writing**. Together they accounted for about **82%** of all finite verb forms in the study. Present perfect and past perfect were much less frequent, but they carried important rhetorical meanings.

## The 12 recurring tense-use cases

### Case 1. Completed action in the current dissertation

Use **simple past** when describing what you did after the research is complete.

Typical locations: abstract, methodology, results.

Examples:

- This study examined three datasets.
- The interviews were conducted in two stages.
- The transcripts were coded thematically.
- The analysis revealed four recurring patterns.

Flag:

- This study examines three datasets.  
  Acceptable in some abstracts and introductions, but in the methodology/results sections of a completed dissertation, simple past is normally stronger.

### Case 2. Completed procedure or instrument use

Use **simple past**, often in passive voice, for concrete research procedures.

Examples:

- Participants were recruited through university mailing lists.
- Responses were analysed using thematic coding.
- The survey was piloted before the main study.

Do not use present tense unless you are describing a standard procedure, a figure/table, or the structure of the dissertation.

### Case 3. Results as findings obtained from completed analysis

Use **simple past** when the clause reports what your analysis found.

Examples:

- The results showed a significant difference between the two groups.
- The interviews revealed three forms of learner resistance.
- The corpus contained 186 relevant instances.

However, switch to **simple present** when interpreting the meaning, implication, or current relevance of the result.

Examples:

- These findings suggest that feedback uptake is mediated by learner agency.
- This pattern indicates a mismatch between institutional policy and classroom practice.

### Case 4. Table, figure, chapter, and section reference

Use **simple present** because the table, figure, or chapter is present to the reader at the time of reading.

Examples:

- Table 4.2 shows the distribution of responses.
- Figure 3 illustrates the coding process.
- Chapter 5 discusses the implications of these findings.

Avoid unnecessary future tense in a completed dissertation.

Better:

- The next chapter discusses the findings.

Usually avoid:

- The next chapter will discuss the findings.

### Case 5. Specific previous study or dated finding

Use **simple past** when referring to a specific completed study, especially with a date, named dataset, named sample, or bounded historical context.

Examples:

- Smith (2019) found that students preferred written feedback.
- Earlier classroom studies reported similar patterns.
- The intervention increased participation in the experimental group.

Common error:

- In 2019, Smith has found that students preferred written feedback.

Correction:

- In 2019, Smith found that students preferred written feedback.

Use present perfect with specific dates only when the date marks the beginning of a period that continues to the present, normally with **since**.

Example:

- Since 2019, researchers have increasingly examined feedback literacy.

### Case 6. Body of research with continuing relevance

Use **present perfect** when previous research forms an ongoing line of inquiry or remains directly relevant now.

Examples:

- Researchers have examined the role of corrective feedback in second-language writing.
- Several studies have shown that learner uptake varies across contexts.
- Few studies have addressed short-term learners in naturalistic settings.

This tense is especially useful for gap statements because it connects the past literature to the present need for your study.

### Case 7. Established knowledge, theoretical claim, or current interpretation

Use **simple present** when presenting a general claim, a theoretical position, a definition, or your current interpretation.

Examples:

- Feedback literacy refers to learners’ capacity to interpret and use feedback.
- Sociocultural theory emphasizes mediation and interaction.
- This finding supports the claim that learning is context-sensitive.

In literature review writing, present tense can indicate that the cited argument remains live.

Example:

- Hyland argues that academic writing is shaped by disciplinary communities.

### Case 8. Earlier-than-past sequencing

Use **past perfect** only when one past event clearly occurred before another past reference point.

Examples:

- Before the interviews were conducted, participants had completed a background questionnaire.
- By the time the second coding cycle began, the initial themes had already been revised.

Do not use past perfect merely because an event is old. Without a second past reference point, use simple past.

Usually wrong:

- Smith had argued that feedback was dialogic.

Better, if it is a specific completed study:

- Smith argued that feedback was dialogic.

Better, if the argument is still treated as active:

- Smith argues that feedback is dialogic.

### Case 9. Historical background

Use **simple past** for bounded historical events, policy changes, institutional actions, or previous conditions.

Examples:

- The university introduced the policy in 2018.
- The program expanded after the curriculum reform.
- Early studies focused mainly on grammar correction.

Use **past perfect** only when clarifying that one historical event preceded another past event.

Example:

- By the time the policy was revised, several departments had already adopted local guidelines.

### Case 10. Literature-gap statement

Use **present perfect** or **simple present**, depending on emphasis.

Use **present perfect** to show that the gap extends from past research into the present.

Examples:

- Few studies have investigated feedback practices among short-term learners.
- Research has not yet explained why similar interventions produce divergent outcomes.

Use **simple present** to state the current condition of the field.

Examples:

- The literature remains fragmented.
- Existing studies provide limited insight into learner-level variation.

### Case 11. Discussion, contribution, and implication

Use a controlled mixture.

Use **simple past** to restate what the study found:

- This study found that learners used feedback selectively.

Use **present perfect** to foreground the current significance of the completed study:

- This study has shown that learner engagement depends on task design.

Use **simple present** to state interpretation, contribution, or implication:

- These findings extend current accounts of feedback literacy.
- The model explains why similar feedback practices generate different learner responses.

This is the most important tense shift in the discussion chapter: past for findings, present perfect for achieved contribution, present for interpretation.

### Case 12. Limitations, recommendations, and future work

Use **simple present** for current limitations:

- This study is limited by its small sample size.
- The findings do not capture long-term development.

Use **modal verbs** or cautious future expressions for recommendations and future research:

- Future research should examine delayed effects.
- Larger longitudinal studies may clarify this relationship.
- Further research is needed to test the model in other contexts.

Avoid overusing **will** unless you are writing a proposal or making a concrete prediction.

## Chapter-by-chapter tense map

### Abstract

Common pattern:

- Background: simple present or present perfect.
- Purpose: simple past or simple present.
- Method: simple past.
- Results: simple past.
- Conclusion/implication: simple present or present perfect.

Example pattern:

- Previous research has examined X, but little attention has been paid to Y. This study investigated Y through semi-structured interviews. The analysis identified three themes. These findings suggest that Y is shaped by Z.

### Introduction

Common pattern:

- General problem: simple present.
- Research history: present perfect or simple past.
- Specific previous study: simple past.
- Gap: present perfect or simple present.
- Aim/research questions: simple present or simple past, depending on institutional style.
- Thesis structure: simple present.

Preferred checking question: Is the clause building the current problem, reporting a past study, or announcing the present dissertation?

### Literature review

Common pattern:

- Theories and definitions: simple present.
- Specific completed studies: simple past.
- Bodies of research: present perfect.
- Gaps and unresolved issues: present perfect or simple present.
- Historical development of the field: simple past.

Do not put the entire literature review in simple past. That makes the field look dead. Use present perfect and present tense to show which ideas remain active.

### Methodology

Common pattern:

- Completed research actions: simple past.
- Rationale for methodological choices: simple present.
- Established method descriptions: simple present.
- Pilot work and data collection: simple past.
- Earlier-than-past sequence: past perfect only when needed.

Example:

- The study used a qualitative design. Interviews were conducted after participants had completed the course. This design allows close analysis of learner experience.

### Results

Common pattern:

- Table/figure location: simple present.
- Procedure or analysis result: simple past.
- Interpretation/commentary: simple present.

Example:

- Table 5 shows the distribution of responses. The first coding cycle identified four categories. This distribution indicates that learners interpreted feedback unevenly.

### Discussion

Common pattern:

- Restated findings: simple past.
- Contribution of the completed study: present perfect.
- Interpretation and implications: simple present.
- Comparison with previous specific studies: simple past.
- Field-level relevance: present perfect or simple present.

Example:

- The study found that learners used feedback selectively. This finding supports a view of feedback uptake as context-dependent. Previous studies have emphasized teacher feedback design, but fewer studies have examined learner interpretation.

### Conclusion

Common pattern:

- Summary of completed study: simple past or present perfect.
- Final claim/contribution: simple present.
- Limitations: simple present.
- Recommendations: modal verbs, cautious future expressions.

Example:

- This dissertation has shown that feedback uptake is shaped by learner agency and task design. The study was limited to one institutional context. Future research should test the model across longer learning periods.

## Fast decision tree

Ask these questions in order:

1. Is the clause describing an action completed in the study?
   - Use simple past.

2. Is it describing a specific previous study, event, or dated finding?
   - Use simple past.

3. Is it describing a body of research that remains relevant or incomplete?
   - Use present perfect.

4. Is it stating a theory, concept, interpretation, implication, table reference, or chapter reference?
   - Use simple present.

5. Is it placing one past event before another past event?
   - Use past perfect.

6. Is it describing ongoing change around the present?
   - Use present progressive.

7. Is it recommending future research or action?
   - Use modal verbs such as should, may, could, or is needed; use will only for concrete future statements.

## High-risk tense errors to flag

### 1. Present perfect with a finished time marker

Problem:

- In 2020, Lee has found that peer feedback improved revision quality.

Correction:

- In 2020, Lee found that peer feedback improved revision quality.

### 2. Past perfect without a past reference point

Problem:

- Previous studies had examined learner anxiety.

Correction:

- Previous studies examined learner anxiety.
- Previous studies have examined learner anxiety.

Use past perfect only if another past point is active:

- Before the present study was conducted, previous studies had examined learner anxiety mainly in test settings.

### 3. Unmotivated switching within the same rhetorical function

Problem:

- The interviews were recorded, transcribed, and are coded thematically.

Correction:

- The interviews were recorded, transcribed, and coded thematically.

### 4. Present tense for completed data collection

Problem:

- The researcher interviews 20 students and analyses their responses.

Correction:

- The researcher interviewed 20 students and analysed their responses.

### 5. Future tense in a completed dissertation

Problem:

- Chapter 4 will present the findings.

Correction:

- Chapter 4 presents the findings.

### 6. Treating all cited literature as past

Problem:

- Vygotsky argued that learning was socially mediated.

Possible correction, if used as a live theoretical claim:

- Vygotsky argues that learning is socially mediated.

Use simple past only when the historical act of arguing is the focus.

### 7. Confusing results and implications

Problem:

- The analysis shows that 37 participants selected option A. This suggested that learners value clarity.

Better:

- The analysis showed that 37 participants selected option A. This suggests that learners value clarity.

### 8. Overusing present perfect for individual studies

Problem:

- Smith (2017) has interviewed 25 students.

Correction:

- Smith (2017) interviewed 25 students.

Present perfect is better for plural or field-level claims:

- Several studies have interviewed students about feedback experience.

## Output format for a tense audit

When checking a dissertation passage, use this table:

| Sentence / clause | Current tense | Rhetorical function | Recommended tense | Revision | Reason |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| ... | ... | completed method / specific previous study / live theory / field gap / implication | ... | ... | ... |

After the table, provide a short verdict:

- **Stable**: tense choices are coherent.
- **Mostly stable, minor shifts needed**: revise local inconsistency.
- **Unstable**: tense choices obscure time sequence or rhetorical function.

---

## Minimal correction policy

Do not rewrite tense unless there is a clear reason. Some tense variation is correct and necessary. The goal is not uniformity; the goal is controlled alternation.

Prioritize corrections in this order:

1. Present perfect + finished date.
2. Past perfect without a past reference point.
3. Present tense used for completed methods/results.
4. Inconsistent tense inside a single list or method sequence.
5. Literature review tense that misrepresents whether a claim is historical, current, or part of an ongoing field.
6. Future tense used for chapter signposting in a completed dissertation.

---

## Compact prompt for using this skill

Use the Reference-Point Model to audit tense in the following dissertation passage. For each clause with a finite verb, identify the current tense, rhetorical function, recommended tense, and whether revision is needed. Focus especially on simple past, present perfect, and past perfect. Do not enforce one tense across the paragraph; judge tense by communicative purpose. Return a table plus a short verdict.
